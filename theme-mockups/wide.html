Hexo & VSCode

2022-05-28

There is something deeply satisfying about stripping away every layer of abstraction until you are left with nothing but text files and a build step. That is what drew me to Hexo in the first place. No database, no admin panel, no WYSIWYG editor fighting you at every turn. Just Markdown, a theme, and a static site generator that does exactly what it says it will do.

Setting it up inside VSCode turned the whole workflow into something genuinely pleasant. The integrated terminal means I can generate a new post, write it, preview it, and deploy it without ever leaving the editor. The Markdown preview pane sits beside my source and updates in real time. Snippets handle the front matter. It is the kind of setup that makes you want to write more, not less.

The best blogging setup is the one that gets out of your way and lets you focus on putting words on a screen. Everything else is ceremony.

The theme is a modified version of Cactus, stripped down and rebuilt to feel like a terminal rather than a website. Dark background, monospace type, minimal navigation. Every visual choice is a deliberate rejection of the idea that a personal site needs to look like a SaaS landing page. It should look like it belongs to the person who made it.

Deployment is a single script. Docker builds the image, Nginx serves the files, and a symlink swap makes the cutover atomic. There is no CI/CD pipeline because there does not need to be one. The entire process from writing to publishing takes under a minute, and most of that time is spent thinking about what to say next.
